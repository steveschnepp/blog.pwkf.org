The hardest judgment call in engineering is not technical. It is knowing which
battles to fight and which to absorb. I have wasted more time than I care to
admit pushing against things that were never going to change — a legacy
architecture, an org decision already made, a library chosen by someone who
left three years ago. And I have let slide things I should have fixed while I
still had leverage.

The Serenity Prayer maps onto this better than most engineering frameworks do.

## Original Version

```
God, grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change.
Courage to change the things I can.
And wisdom to know the difference.
```

The invocation of God is not essential to the structure. Yet people with a
different belief system often dismiss it on that basis alone, which is
unfortunate — the useful part is the three-way split, not the addressee.

## A Secular Adaptation

```
May calm rise within me for all that lies beyond my reach.
May courage awaken in me for all that calls for action.
And may clarity guide me in knowing the difference.
```

The third line is the hard one. Serenity and courage are attitudes. Clarity is
a skill. It is the one that degrades under deadline pressure, politics, and
accumulated fatigue. It is also the one most worth deliberately cultivating.

In practice: if you cannot name *specifically* what would change if you fixed
it, you are probably looking at something beyond your reach. If you can name
it, you probably can change it — the question is whether it is worth the cost
right now.

That is not a perfect heuristic. But it is a faster one than most.
